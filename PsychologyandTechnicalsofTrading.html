Trading is not solely about technical analysis; rather, it is primarily influenced by psychology and money management.

 

Psychology and money management rules / checklist

 

Your trading account (including equity, funds and derivatives) should not exceed 20% of your total net worth. This also depends on your age and risk profile. Consult a financial advisor for right asset allocation.

Not more than 1% of your trading capital should be used for derivative trading at any point in time. That is 0.2% of your net worth.

Technicals

 

P.S. There are many other technical parameters like volume etc but I always felt comfortable trading based on the following guidelines.

 

A position that doesn’t move into money within 30 minutes, sometimes even within 30 seconds, seldom makes profit until the final hour of trading, and may never do so at closing. 

 

How to play :

 

  • You know you have a winner or loser right from the beginning.
  • Do not average losses especially in derivatives.
  • You can average profits in a trending market (strong bull or strong bear phase).
  • Avoid averaging profits in a sideways market.
  • One buy, one sell at a profit (or at a stop loss) is the way to go at any point in time.

 

Before beginning to trade a stock or index, it's essential to understand the following indicators:

 

  • 20-day moving average (20 DMA)
  • 50-day moving average (50 DMA)
  • 100-day moving average (100 DMA)
  • 200-day moving average (200 DMA)
  • 5-day high/low
  • 1-month high/low
  • 1-year high/low
  •  

The above are major support and resistance levels that can act as entry or exit points. Google Finance, Yahoo Finance, Trading View - all major sites providing charts show these technical parameters. It's better to write these down and keep them in front of you when you are in a trade.

No trade is guaranteed, and you should always have a stop loss set 2 or 3 percent below your entry point. These are some high-probability scenarios based on my observations.

 

60 to 70 % times CALL / Buy if markets above major moving averages (50/100/200 DMA)

 

60 to 70 % times PUT or Sell if markets below major moving averages (50/100/200 DMA)

 

“Window Dressing” period and trading : During sideways and bull phases (when trading above the 52-week high), it is advisable to refrain from short selling in the last two trading days of a month or quarter, as large-cap funds tend to buy their own stock to show better performance. Conversely, during bear phases (when trading below the 52-week low), it's best to avoid long trades on the last two trading days of months and quarters, as bears typically exert control, and net short funds may amplify their gains by selling more.

 

Stay on top of the news flow, but it's better to mute the TV during trading hours to avoid being influenced by other analysts' views. 

 

The absence of bad news is typically seen as positive for the market, given its inherent upward bias. In a broader context, the investment universe tends to be predominantly long-only, with institutions and long-only funds typically avoiding large-scale sell-offs, as they maintain a certain maximum percentage of cash levels. Therefore, it's advisable to refrain from shorting the market during sideways movements unless technical indicators suggest a strong sell zone.

 

If you find yourself uncertain about trading decisions, it's generally advisable to consider buying CALL options or staying long during a bull phase (trading above 52 week high ) more frequently. Conversely, consider buying PUT options or going short more often during a bear phase ( trading below 52 week low).
